Arya was an ordinary boy in an ordinary town.



He studied in the ninth standard, sat on the second bench by the window, and had the kind of sharp mind teachers quietly admired. Numbers made sense to him. Words stayed where he put them. Life, until recently, followed a simple rhythm: school, home, homework, sleep.



Then it began.



At first, it was strange rather than frightening.



Two seniors would suddenly walk into his classroom in the middle of a lecture. They never asked the teacher for permission. They never looked at anyone else.



They looked only at him.



“Hey. What’s your name?” one of them would shout.



“A-Arya,” he would reply, confused.



That was enough.



They would smirk, whisper something to each other, and then it would start. Threats at first, murmured just loud enough for him to hear. Then pushing in the corridors. Rumours spread so quietly he only realised them when classmates stopped sitting near him. A shove on the stairs. A punch that looked like an accident.



Arya couldn’t understand why.



He hadn’t fought anyone. He hadn’t spoken against anyone. He hadn’t even noticed the seniors before they noticed him.



The fear settled in slowly, like dampness in walls.



One afternoon, when the school was unusually quiet, two seniors entered his class.



Everyone knew them.



One was notorious, famous for never being punished. The other was worse, silent, ruthless, with eyes that didn’t blink much. They belonged to the strongest bully gang in the town. Even teachers avoided crossing them.



Arya felt his stomach drop.



“What’s your name?” the notorious one asked.



Arya didn’t answer.



His legs moved on their own. He slipped out through the back door, heart hammering, hoping, just hoping to disappear.



A hand grabbed his collar.



It was one of his own classmates.



“I got him,” the boy said nervously, as if trying to prove something.



Arya froze.



They dragged him behind the building. Slaps came first, sharp and sudden. Then laughter. Someone kicked him lightly, testing how far they could go.



The notorious one leaned close, his breath warm against Arya’s ear.



“Can we have it?”



Arya blinked through tears.

“Have… what?”



No answer came. They simply laughed and walked away, as if they had already said enough.



That night, Arya lay awake, staring at the fan slicing the darkness above him.



The question echoed in his head.



Can we have it?



Suddenly, a memory surfaced.



Six months ago.



He had been standing near a small shop when a man he didn’t know had approached him. The man had looked ordinary, almost forgettable. He had smiled strangely and asked the same thing.



“Can we have it?”



Arya had thought it was a joke. Or a mistake.



Now it didn’t feel like either.



What do I have, Arya wondered, that they want?



The answer didn’t come.



A few days later, it happened.



The van stopped suddenly. Hands grabbed him. A cloth covered his mouth. The world became noise and darkness.



When the cloth was removed, Arya found himself in an isolated open area. Dust. Silence. And parked nearby, several black luxury cars, their polished surfaces reflecting the sky like mirrors.



He was thrown to the ground. Someone kicked him, carefully, methodically. Pain bloomed, but nothing broke.



A man stepped out of one of the cars.



He was in his late forties. Calm. Well-dressed. Powerful.



The man placed his foot on Arya’s hand.



The pain was immediate and unbearable.



He squatted down and looked Arya straight in the eyes.



“Can we have it?”



“I don’t know what you want!” Arya screamed, tears spilling freely now.



The man sighed, almost disappointed.



“We want that property in your name.”



Arya stared at him.

“I don’t have any property.”



The man nodded and pulled out a thick government register. He opened it and placed it in front of Arya’s face.



“This one,” he said.



Arya’s name was written clearly.



Below it: details he couldn’t understand.



Land.



2100 acres.



Located in a major city.



Registered in his name.



On the day he was born.



Arya’s mind went blank.



“This is impossible,” he whispered. “My father is a government employee. He earns a basic salary. We only have our house.”



The man smiled faintly.



“Give us the property,” he said. “And you walk away.”



Arya shook his head, helpless.



“I don’t know anything about this.”



The man stood up.



“This is your final warning.”



They let him go.



When Arya reached home, his body ached, but there were no visible scars. They had been careful.



He sat with his father during dinner, watching him talk about work, about bills, about ordinary things.



Trying to sound casual, Arya asked, “Papa… do I have any property in my name?”



His father laughed.

“Property? I only have this house. Nothing else.”



That night, Arya lay in bed, staring at the ceiling.



The fan hummed.



The question returned, heavier than before.



What was registered in my name… and why?



The ceiling gave no answers.

Only silence.
